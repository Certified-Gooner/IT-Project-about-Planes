Boeing

Founded: July 15, 1916, by William E. Boeing in Seattle, Washington.

Headquarters: Crystal City, Virginia, United States.

Employees: Approximately 170,688 worldwide as of 2023.

Revenue: $77.79 billion in 2023.

Business Segments:

-Commercial Airplanes

-Defense, Space & Security

-Global Services


707

Type: Narrow-body airliner.

First Flight: December 20, 1957.

Entry into Service: October 26, 1958, with Pan American World Airways (Pan Am).

Manufacturer: Boeing Commercial Airplanes.

Design: Four-engine, swept-wing jet airliner.

Speed: Capable of cruising at speeds around 550 mph (885 km/h).

Range: Approximately 3,000 nautical miles (5,556 kilometers).

Capacity: Typically accommodated around 150 passengers, depending on the configuration.

Variants:

707-120: Initial production version.

707-220: Longer-range version with more powerful engines.

707-320: Further stretched version with increased range.

707-420: Version with Rolls-Royce Conway turbofans.

707-700: Military version used for air refueling and transport.

Notable Features:

First successful commercial jetliner.

Played a crucial role in transforming long-distance air travel by significantly reducing flight times.

Known for its spacious cabin and advanced turbojet engines.

Operational History: Dominated passenger air transport in the 1960s and 1970s, used for both commercial and military purposes.

Retirement: Most commercial 707s were retired by the late 1980s, but some military variants remain in service



737

Type: Narrow-body twin-engine jet airliner.

First Flight: April 9, 1967.

Entry into Service: February 10, 1968, with Lufthansa.

Manufacturer: Boeing Commercial Airplanes.

Variants: The 737 family includes multiple generations and variants:

Original Series: 737-100, 737-200.

Classic Series: 737-300, 737-400, 737-500.

Next Generation (NG) Series: 737-600, 737-700, 737-800, 737-900.

737 MAX Series: 737 MAX 7, 737 MAX 8, 737 MAX 9, 737 MAX 10.

Capacity: Ranges from 85 to 230 passengers depending on the variant and configuration.

Range:

Next Generation Series: Approximately 3,000 to 3,500 nautical miles (5,556 to 6,482 kilometers).

MAX Series: Approximately 3,215 to 3,850 nautical miles (5,954 to 7,130 kilometers).

Engines: CFM56-7B for NG Series, LEAP-1B for MAX Series.

Notable Features:

Known for its reliability and efficiency.

Widely used by airlines worldwide for short to medium-haul routes.

Advanced avionics and passenger comfort features in newer models.

Operational History: Over 10,000 units produced, making it the best-selling commercial jetliner in history.
Recent Developments: The 737 MAX series features improved fuel efficiency, lower emissions, and advanced technology, but experienced a temporary grounding in 2019-2020 due to safety concerns, which have since been addressed with regulatory approval for a return to service.



747

Type: Wide-body, four-engine jet airliner.

First Flight: February 9, 1969.

Entry into Service: January 22, 1970, with Pan American World Airways (Pan Am).

Manufacturer: Boeing Commercial Airplanes.

Design: Features a distinctive humpbacked upper deck, initially designed to accommodate a first-class lounge or extra seating.

Variants: The 747 family includes several key variants:

747-100: The original version, introduced in 1970.

747-200: Improved range and payload capabilities, introduced in 1971.

747-300: Extended upper deck, introduced in 1983.

747-400: Advanced version with improved engines and winglets, introduced in 1989.

747-8: The latest and largest version, with modernized features and increased efficiency, introduced in 2011.

Capacity: Can typically seat between 366 and 524 passengers, depending on the configuration and variant.

Range: Varies by variant; the 747-400 has a range of approximately 7,260 nautical miles (13,450 kilometers), while the 747-8 can fly up to 7,730 nautical miles (14,320 kilometers).

Speed: Cruising speed of Mach 0.85 (570 mph, 920 km/h).

Engines:

747-100/200/300: Pratt & Whitney JT9D, General Electric CF6, or Rolls-Royce RB211.

747-400: Pratt & Whitney PW4000, General Electric CF6, or Rolls-Royce RB211.

747-8: General Electric GEnx-2B67.

Notable Features:

Double-deck configuration.

Four-engine design for long-haul routes.

Known for its impressive size and passenger capacity.

Significant cargo capacity in freighter versions.

Operational History: Used extensively for long-haul flights, cargo transport, and even as Air Force One (the U.S. presidential aircraft).

Retirement: Many airlines have started retiring their 747 fleets in favor of more fuel-efficient twin-engine aircraft, but the 747-8 remains in production for cargo and some passenger operations.



767

Type: Wide-body twin-engine jet airliner.

First Flight: September 26, 1981.

Entry into Service: September 8, 1982, with United Airlines.

Manufacturer: Boeing Commercial Airplanes.

Variants: The 767 family includes several key variants:

767-200: The initial version, designed for medium to long-haul routes.

767-300: A stretched version with increased passenger capacity, introduced in 1986.

767-300ER: Extended range version, introduced in 1988.

767-400ER: Further stretched with more capacity and range, introduced in 2000.

767 Freighter: Cargo version of the aircraft.

KC-46 Pegasus: Military aerial refueling and transport version.

Capacity:

767-200: Typically seats 181 to 224 passengers.

767-300: Typically seats 218 to 269 passengers.

767-400ER: Typically seats 245 to 304 passengers.

Range:

767-200ER: Up to 6,590 nautical miles (12,200 kilometers).

767-300ER: Up to 5,990 nautical miles (11,070 kilometers).

767-400ER: Up to 5,625 nautical miles (10,415 kilometers).

Engines: Options include Pratt & Whitney JT9D, General Electric CF6, or Rolls-Royce RB211.

Notable Features:

Advanced aerodynamics and efficient engines.

Wide-body design for increased passenger comfort.

Versatile for both passenger and cargo operations.

Fly-by-wire system for improved control and safety.

Operational History: Widely used by airlines around the world for medium to long-haul routes, and also adapted for military and cargo operations.



787 Dreamliner

Type: Long-haul, wide-body, twin-engine jet airliner.

First Flight: December 15, 2009.

Entry into Service: October 26, 2011, with All Nippon Airways (ANA).

Manufacturer: Boeing Commercial Airplanes.

Variants: The 787 family includes three main variants:

787-8: The base model, designed for medium to long-haul routes.

787-9: A stretched version with more passenger capacity and range.

787-10: The longest version, optimized for maximum passenger capacity and efficiency on shorter routes.

Capacity:

787-8: Typically seats 242 passengers in a two-class configuration.

787-9: Typically seats 290 passengers in a two-class configuration.

787-10: Typically seats 330 passengers in a two-class configuration.

Range:

787-8: Up to 7,530 nautical miles (13,950 kilometers).

787-9: Up to 7,530 nautical miles (14,140 kilometers).

787-10: Up to 6,430 nautical miles (11,910 kilometers).

Engines: Options include Rolls-Royce Trent 1000 or General Electric GEnx.

Notable Features:

Advanced composite materials make up 50% of the primary structure, reducing weight and increasing efficiency.

Larger windows and improved cabin pressure for enhanced passenger comfort.

LED lighting, higher humidity, and improved air filtration for a more comfortable cabin environment.

Quiet and fuel-efficient engines, reducing operational costs and environmental impact.

Operational History: Used by airlines around the world for a variety of routes, praised for its fuel efficiency and passenger comfort.

Production: Manufactured in Boeing's facilities in Everett, Washington, and North Charleston, South Carolina.
