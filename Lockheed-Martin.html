Lockheed Martin

Founded: March 15, 1995, through a merger of Lockheed Corporation and Martin Marietta.

Headquarters: Bethesda, Maryland, United States.

Employees: Approximately 122,000 worldwide, including about 60,000 engineers and scientists.

Revenue: $67.6 billion in 2023.

Business Segments:

-Aeronautics
-Missiles and Fire Control
-Rotary and Mission Systems
-Space


F-22 Raptor

Type: Air superiority fighter.

First Flight: September 7, 1997.

Entry into Service: December 15, 2005.

Manufacturer: Lockheed Martin, with Boeing as a major subcontractor.

Design: Twin-engine, all-weather, supersonic stealth fighter.

Speed: Capable of speeds greater than Mach 2 with afterburners and Mach 1.8 at military power.

Range: Approximately 1,840 nautical miles (3,360 kilometers) without refueling.

Armament: Equipped with a 20mm M61A2 Vulcan cannon and can carry a variety of air-to-air missiles, including AIM-120 AMRAAM and AIM-9 Sidewinder.

Stealth: Advanced stealth technology to reduce radar cross-section and infrared signature.

Avionics: Integrated avionics system with sensor fusion for enhanced situational awareness.

Operational History: Used in various missions, including air superiority, ground attack, electronic warfare, and signals intelligence.

Production: 195 aircraft built (8 test and 187 production aircraft).

Current Status: Remains a critical component of the USAF's tactical airpower, with plans to keep it in service until it is succeeded by the Next Generation Air Dominance (NGAD) fighter



F-35 Lightning

Type: Multirole strike fighter.

First Flight: December 15, 2006.

Entry into Service:

F-35A: August 2, 2016 (USAF).

F-35B: July 31, 2015 (USMC).

F-35C: February 28, 2019 (USN).

Manufacturer: Lockheed Martin, with principal partners Northrop Grumman and BAE Systems.

Variants:

F-35A: Conventional takeoff and landing (CTOL).

F-35B: Short takeoff and vertical landing (STOVL).

F-35C: Carrier-based (CV/CATOBAR).

Speed: Capable of supersonic speeds (Mach 1.6+).

Range: Approximately 1,200 nautical miles (2,200 kilometers) on internal fuel.

Armament: Equipped with a 25mm GAU-22/A cannon and can carry a variety of air-to-air and air-to-ground missiles3.

Stealth: Advanced stealth technology to reduce radar cross-section and infrared signature.

Avionics: Integrated avionics system with sensor fusion for enhanced situational awareness.

Operational History: Used by the United States Air Force, Navy, and Marine Corps, as well as several allied nations.
Production: Over 1,000 aircraft built and more on order.

Notable Features:

Advanced sensors and information fusion.

Network-enabled operations.

High maneuverability and supersonic capabilities.

Designed for both air superiority and strike missions



C-5 Galaxy

Type: Strategic airlift aircraft.

First Flight: June 30, 1968.

Entry into Service: June 1970 with the United States Air Force (USAF).

Manufacturer: Lockheed Martin.

Variants:

C-5A: Initial production version.

C-5B: Improved version with upgraded avionics and structural improvements.

C-5C: Modified version for transporting large cargo.

C-5M Super Galaxy: Modernized version with upgraded engines and avionics.

Length: 75.5 meters (247 feet 1 inch).

Wingspan: 67.89 meters (222 feet 9 inches).

Height: 19.84 meters (65 feet 1 inch).

Maximum Takeoff Weight: 381,000 kilograms (840,000 pounds).

Cargo Capacity: Can carry up to 130,000 kilograms (286,000 pounds) of cargo.

Range: Approximately 5,500 nautical miles (10,200 kilometers) with a maximum payload.

Speed: Cruise speed of Mach 0.77 (528 mph, 850 km/h).

Engines: Four General Electric CF6-80C2 engines (for the C-5M Super Galaxy).

Notable Features:

High-wing design with a distinctive T-tail.

Nose and aft cargo doors allow for "drive-on/drive-off" loading and unloading.

Ability to kneel for easier loading and unloading of cargo.

Extensive range and payload capacity for strategic airlift missions.

Operational History: Used in various military operations for transporting troops, equipment, and supplies. It's known for its ability to carry oversized cargo such as tanks and helicopters.



SR-71 Blackbird

Type: Strategic reconnaissance aircraft.

First Flight: December 22, 1964.

Entry into Service: January 1966 with the United States Air Force (USAF).

Manufacturer: Lockheed Corporation.

Design: Twin-engine, long-range, high-altitude aircraft capable of speeds over Mach 3 (three times the speed of sound).

Speed: Capable of sustained speeds of Mach 3.2 (approximately 2,200 mph or 3,540 km/h).

Altitude: Operated at altitudes up to 85,000 feet (26,000 meters).

Range: Approximately 2,900 nautical miles (5,400 kilometers) with a full fuel load.

Armament: Equipped with a 20mm M61A1 Vulcan cannon for self-defense.

Stealth: Advanced design to minimize radar cross-section and infrared signature.

Operational History: Used for strategic reconnaissance missions during the Cold War, including overflights of hostile territories.

Production: A total of 32 aircraft were built, including 29 SR-71A and 2 SR-71B (trainer versions).

Retirement: The USAF retired the SR-71 in 1998, but NASA continued to use it for research until 1999.

Notable Features:

Built primarily from titanium to withstand high temperatures generated by air friction at high speeds.

Known for its ability to outrun threats, including surface-to-air missiles.

Set numerous speed and altitude records during its operational history.

Current Status: All SR-71s are now retired and displayed in museums around the world.
